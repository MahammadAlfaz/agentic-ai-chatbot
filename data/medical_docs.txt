FEVER
=====
Fever is a medical condition where the body temperature rises above the normal range (98.6°F or 37°C), usually as a response to infection or illness. Fever is not a disease itself but a symptom that indicates the body is fighting something.

Causes:
- Viral infections (flu, COVID-19, common cold)
- Bacterial infections (pneumonia, urinary tract infection, strep throat)
- Inflammatory conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, lupus)
- Heat exhaustion or heat stroke
- Certain medications (drug-induced fever)
- Vaccinations (mild fever as immune response)

Symptoms:
- Body temperature above 100.4°F (38°C)
- Sweating and chills
- Headache
- Muscle aches and weakness
- Loss of appetite
- Dehydration
- General feeling of being unwell

Treatment:
- Rest and adequate fluid intake
- Over-the-counter medications: paracetamol (acetaminophen) or ibuprofen
- Lukewarm sponge bath to reduce temperature
- Light clothing and cool environment
- Medical attention if fever exceeds 103°F or lasts more than 3 days

Prevention:
- Regular handwashing
- Vaccination
- Avoiding close contact with sick individuals
- Maintaining good hygiene

When to See a Doctor:
- Fever above 103°F (39.4°C)
- Fever lasting more than 3 days
- Severe headache or stiff neck
- Difficulty breathing
- Rash or unusual symptoms
- Fever in infants under 3 months


FLU (INFLUENZA)
===============
Influenza (flu) is a highly contagious viral infection that primarily affects the respiratory system including the nose, throat, and lungs. It is caused by influenza viruses type A and B.

Causes:
- Influenza A virus (most common, causes seasonal epidemics)
- Influenza B virus (less severe, mainly affects humans)
- Spread through respiratory droplets when infected person coughs or sneezes
- Contact with contaminated surfaces

Symptoms:
- Sudden onset of high fever (100-104°F)
- Severe body aches and muscle pain
- Fatigue and weakness
- Dry cough
- Sore throat
- Runny or stuffy nose
- Headache
- Chills
- Vomiting and diarrhea (more common in children)

Complications:
- Pneumonia
- Bronchitis
- Sinus infections
- Ear infections
- Worsening of chronic conditions (asthma, heart disease)

Treatment:
- Rest and hydration
- Antiviral medications (oseltamivir/Tamiflu, zanamivir/Relenza) — most effective within 48 hours
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen for fever and pain
- Avoid aspirin in children (risk of Reye's syndrome)

Prevention:
- Annual flu vaccination (most effective prevention)
- Frequent handwashing
- Covering mouth when coughing or sneezing
- Avoiding crowded places during flu season
- Staying home when sick

Recovery:
- Most people recover within 1-2 weeks
- Fatigue may persist for several weeks
- High-risk groups (elderly, pregnant, immunocompromised) need extra care


COVID-19
========
COVID-19 is an infectious disease caused by the SARS-CoV-2 coronavirus, first identified in December 2019. It primarily spreads through respiratory droplets and aerosols.

Causes:
- SARS-CoV-2 coronavirus
- Spread through respiratory droplets and aerosols
- Close contact with infected individuals
- Contact with contaminated surfaces (less common)

Symptoms (Mild to Moderate):
- Fever or chills
- Dry cough
- Fatigue and tiredness
- Loss of taste or smell (anosmia)
- Sore throat
- Headache
- Muscle or body aches
- Runny nose
- Nausea or vomiting
- Diarrhea

Symptoms (Severe):
- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
- Persistent chest pain or pressure
- Confusion or inability to stay awake
- Bluish lips or face (cyanosis)
- Low oxygen saturation

Complications:
- Pneumonia
- Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)
- Multi-organ failure
- Blood clots
- Long COVID (persistent symptoms for weeks or months)

Treatment:
- Mild cases: rest, hydration, paracetamol for fever
- Antiviral medications: nirmatrelvir/ritonavir (Paxlovid) for high-risk patients
- Severe cases: hospitalization, oxygen therapy, mechanical ventilation
- Corticosteroids (dexamethasone) for severe inflammation

Prevention:
- COVID-19 vaccination (primary prevention)
- Wearing masks in crowded indoor spaces
- Hand hygiene
- Ventilation of indoor spaces
- Staying home when symptomatic

Recovery:
- Most recover within 2 weeks
- Long COVID affects 10-30% of patients
- Regular follow-up for severe cases


CHEST PAIN
==========
Chest pain is a common and potentially serious symptom that can range from a dull ache to sharp stabbing pain. It requires prompt medical evaluation as it can indicate serious heart or lung conditions.

Causes (Cardiac):
- Heart attack (myocardial infarction)
- Angina (reduced blood flow to heart)
- Pericarditis (inflammation of heart lining)
- Myocarditis (inflammation of heart muscle)
- Aortic dissection (emergency)

Causes (Pulmonary):
- Pulmonary embolism (blood clot in lungs)
- Pneumonia
- Pleuritis (inflammation of lung lining)
- Pneumothorax (collapsed lung)

Causes (Non-Cardiac):
- Acid reflux or GERD
- Esophageal spasm
- Costochondritis (rib cartilage inflammation)
- Muscle strain
- Anxiety or panic attack
- Shingles

Symptoms That Suggest Serious Cause:
- Crushing or squeezing chest pain
- Pain radiating to arm, jaw, neck, or back
- Shortness of breath
- Sweating
- Nausea
- Dizziness or fainting
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat

Diagnosis:
- ECG (electrocardiogram)
- Blood tests (troponin for heart attack)
- Chest X-ray
- CT scan
- Echocardiogram

Treatment:
- Depends entirely on underlying cause
- Cardiac chest pain: immediate emergency treatment
- GERD: antacids, proton pump inhibitors
- Muscle strain: rest, anti-inflammatory medications
- Always seek immediate medical attention for unexplained chest pain


HEART ATTACK (MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION)
=====================================
A heart attack occurs when blood flow to a section of the heart muscle is blocked for long enough that part of the heart muscle is damaged or dies. It is a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.

Causes:
- Coronary artery disease (plaque buildup in arteries)
- Blood clot forming on ruptured plaque
- Coronary artery spasm (less common)
- Spontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD)

Risk Factors:
- High blood pressure (hypertension)
- High cholesterol (hyperlipidemia)
- Smoking
- Diabetes mellitus
- Obesity
- Family history of heart disease
- Age (men over 45, women over 55)
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Chronic stress

Symptoms:
- Chest pain, pressure, tightness, or squeezing
- Pain radiating to left arm, jaw, neck, or back
- Shortness of breath
- Cold sweat
- Nausea or vomiting
- Lightheadedness or dizziness
- Sudden fatigue
- Women may have atypical symptoms: fatigue, jaw pain, nausea without chest pain

Emergency Treatment:
- Call emergency services immediately (911)
- Chew aspirin (325mg) if not allergic
- Nitroglycerin if prescribed
- CPR if patient is unresponsive
- Defibrillation if in cardiac arrest

Medical Treatment:
- Thrombolytics (clot-busting drugs)
- Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI/angioplasty)
- Coronary artery stenting
- Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery

Medications After Heart Attack:
- Aspirin (antiplatelet)
- Beta blockers
- ACE inhibitors
- Statins (cholesterol lowering)
- Blood thinners (anticoagulants)

Recovery:
- Cardiac rehabilitation program
- Lifestyle changes (diet, exercise, no smoking)
- Regular cardiology follow-up
- Monitoring for complications
- Psychological support (depression common after heart attack)

Prevention:
- Regular exercise (150 minutes per week)
- Heart-healthy diet (low saturated fat, high fiber)
- Quit smoking
- Control blood pressure and cholesterol
- Manage diabetes
- Maintain healthy weight
- Reduce stress
- Regular medical checkups


DIABETES
=========
Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disease characterized by high blood glucose (blood sugar) levels due to problems with insulin production, insulin action, or both.

Types:
- Type 1: Autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing cells
- Type 2: Insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency (most common, 90-95%)
- Gestational: Develops during pregnancy
- MODY: Maturity Onset Diabetes of the Young (rare genetic form)

Causes:
- Type 1: Autoimmune reaction, genetic factors, possibly viral triggers
- Type 2: Obesity, physical inactivity, genetics, poor diet, age

Symptoms:
- Frequent urination (polyuria)
- Excessive thirst (polydipsia)
- Unexplained weight loss
- Increased hunger (polyphagia)
- Fatigue and weakness
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing wounds
- Frequent infections
- Numbness or tingling in hands and feet
- Darkened skin in body creases (acanthosis nigricans — Type 2)

Diagnosis:
- Fasting blood glucose test
- HbA1c test (average blood sugar over 3 months)
- Oral glucose tolerance test
- Random blood glucose test

Complications (Long-term):
- Diabetic retinopathy (eye damage, blindness)
- Diabetic nephropathy (kidney disease)
- Diabetic neuropathy (nerve damage)
- Cardiovascular disease (heart attack, stroke)
- Peripheral artery disease
- Diabetic foot (ulcers, amputation risk)

Treatment (Type 1):
- Insulin therapy (multiple daily injections or insulin pump)
- Blood glucose monitoring
- Carbohydrate counting
- Regular medical follow-up

Treatment (Type 2):
- Lifestyle changes first (diet, exercise, weight loss)
- Oral medications: metformin (first line)
- Other medications: sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors, SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists
- Insulin if other treatments insufficient

Prevention (Type 2):
- Maintain healthy weight
- Regular physical activity
- Healthy diet (low sugar, high fiber)
- Avoid smoking
- Regular blood sugar screening if at risk


PARACETAMOL (ACETAMINOPHEN)
============================
Paracetamol, also known as acetaminophen, is one of the most widely used over-the-counter medications for pain relief and fever reduction. It is considered safe when used as directed.

Uses:
- Mild to moderate pain relief (headaches, toothache, muscle aches, menstrual cramps)
- Fever reduction (antipyretic)
- Pain from colds and flu
- Arthritis pain (mild)
- Post-surgical pain (mild)

Mechanism:
- Blocks pain signals in the central nervous system
- Reduces fever by affecting the hypothalamus heat-regulating center
- Does NOT have significant anti-inflammatory properties

Dosage (Adults):
- Standard dose: 500mg to 1000mg per dose
- Maximum: 4000mg per day (4g)
- Interval: every 4-6 hours
- With or without food

Dosage (Children):
- Based on weight: 10-15mg per kg per dose
- Every 4-6 hours as needed
- Do not exceed 5 doses in 24 hours

Side Effects (Rare at normal doses):
- Nausea
- Stomach pain
- Loss of appetite
- Allergic reactions (rare)

Overdose Warning:
- Liver damage (hepatotoxicity) — most serious risk
- Overdose can be fatal
- Symptoms of overdose: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (initial), then liver failure
- Seek immediate medical attention if overdose suspected
- Antidote: N-acetylcysteine (NAC)

Contraindications:
- Severe liver disease
- Alcoholism (chronic heavy drinking increases liver damage risk)
- Allergy to paracetamol

Drug Interactions:
- Warfarin (blood thinner) — paracetamol may increase bleeding risk
- Alcohol — increases liver toxicity risk
- Other paracetamol-containing products (avoid double dosing)

Special Populations:
- Pregnancy: considered safe at recommended doses
- Breastfeeding: safe
- Elderly: use lowest effective dose
- Liver disease: use with caution, consult doctor


IBUPROFEN
=========
Ibuprofen is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) that reduces pain, fever, and inflammation. It is widely available over the counter and is one of the most commonly used medications worldwide.

Uses:
- Mild to moderate pain (headache, toothache, muscle pain, menstrual cramps)
- Fever reduction
- Inflammation and swelling
- Arthritis (osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis)
- Sports injuries
- Post-operative pain

Mechanism:
- Inhibits COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes
- Reduces production of prostaglandins (chemicals that cause pain and inflammation)
- Has analgesic, antipyretic, and anti-inflammatory properties

Dosage (Adults):
- Pain/Fever: 200-400mg every 4-6 hours
- Arthritis: 400-800mg three to four times daily
- Maximum: 1200mg per day (OTC), 3200mg per day (prescription)
- Take with food or milk to reduce stomach irritation

Dosage (Children):
- 5-10mg per kg per dose
- Every 6-8 hours as needed
- Not recommended for infants under 6 months

Side Effects:
- Stomach upset, nausea, heartburn
- Stomach ulcers and bleeding (with long-term use)
- Increased blood pressure
- Fluid retention
- Kidney problems (with long-term use)
- Increased risk of heart attack and stroke (with long-term high-dose use)
- Allergic reactions (rare)

Contraindications:
- Active stomach ulcer or GI bleeding
- Severe kidney or liver disease
- Heart failure
- Last trimester of pregnancy
- Allergy to NSAIDs or aspirin
- Age under 6 months

Drug Interactions:
- Aspirin (reduces cardioprotective effect of aspirin)
- Blood thinners (warfarin) — increases bleeding risk
- ACE inhibitors and diuretics — reduces their effectiveness
- Lithium — increases lithium levels
- Other NSAIDs — avoid combination

Long-term Use Concerns:
- Stomach ulcers and bleeding
- Kidney damage
- Cardiovascular events
- Regular monitoring required if using long-term

Special Populations:
- Elderly: use lowest effective dose, increased risk of side effects
- Pregnancy: avoid in third trimester, use with caution in first and second
- Kidney disease: use with caution or avoid
